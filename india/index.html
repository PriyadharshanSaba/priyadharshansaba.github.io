A Photo Essay · India · 2025

A Year on the Road

Festivals, Rituals, Roads

Priyadharshan 2025

Foreword

In a country that lives by its calendar of gods, a year is never empty.

This is a slow accounting of a fast year accross India, from the river at Prayagraj to the immersion at the Hooghly, from the cremation ground at Varanasi to the high villages of the Nilgiris.

The festivals here are not events. They are how time is told. Each one carries a memory older than any photograph — and yet asks, every year, to be looked at again.

These are the looks I came back with.

I — February

At the meeting of rivers, the largest gathering on earth.

Prayagraj, Uttar Pradesh

Plate 01 · Maha Kumbh Once every twelve years, the Ganga, Yamuna and the invisible Saraswati braid into one bathing ghat. Twelve such cycles — the span of one full revolution of Jupiter through the zodiac, returned to its origin — mark a Maha Kumbh, last seen in 1881. In 2025 the count crossed sixty crore pilgrims — a city of devotion conjured, lit, and dissolved in forty-five days.

II — March

Holi played in the ash of the dead.

Manikarnika Ghat, Varanasi

Plate 02 · Masaan Ki Holi The day before Holi, Shiva is said to dance at Manikarnika — playing colours not with petals but with the ash of the cremation pyres. The living and the dead share one festival. Life and death, in Varanasi, are not opposites.

III — March

A six-hundred-year-old fire, lit again.

Melur Village, the Nilgiris

Plate 03 · Kenda Habba The Badaga people walk through fire to mark the year. The ritual predates the colonial maps of the Nilgiris by four centuries. The hills change. The fire is the same.

IV — May

Thrissur Pooram — a festival born from exclusion.

Thrissur, Kerala

Plate 04 · Pooram In 1798 the smaller temples of Thrissur were turned away from the Arattupuzha Pooram. Sakthan Thampuran answered with a festival of his own. Two centuries later it still empties a city for a single night. The full essay lives here.

V — June

An eighteen-year wait, broken in an afternoon.

Bengaluru, Karnataka

Plate 05 · Victory Parade Royal Challengers Bengaluru lift their first IPL trophy in eighteen years. The city locks down. Devotion in this country has many altars — some of them are cricket grounds.

VI — June

A chariot too heavy for faith alone.

Puri, Odisha

Plate 06 · Rath Yatra The chariot of Lord Jagannath weighs over two hundred tonnes. Hundreds of thousands pull it down Grand Road. From this festival the English language took a word — juggernaut — and emptied it of its god.

VII — July

In the high villages, the calendar belongs to the harvest.

Kilkundha & Kodai, the Nilgiris

Plates 08 — 09 · Dev Habba The Badagas welcome the harvest with Devhabba — a festival of land, not of temple. The rituals are agrarian, the gods are local, and the songs predate any written record of them.

Plate 07 · A Badaga Wedding The women of the bride's village gather around her before she is walked to the mandapa. The Badaga community has lived in these hills for at least seven hundred years. Their weddings are quiet, and very old.

VIII — July

Where the wildlife path meets the shrine.

The Nilgiris

Plate 10 · A Sloth Bear at a Forest Temple In The Nilgiris of southern India, ancient wildlife paths intersect with places of human devotion. This wild sloth bear (Melursus ursinus) paused beside a small forest shrine worshipped daily by local tribal communities — a space where people and wildlife live through the shared wilderness. Sloth bears here are known to investigate temples for the scent of ghee used to fuel ritual diyas. As development pushes further into biodiverse landscapes, encounters like this reveal the delicate balance between culture and nature — and the hopeful possibility of coexistence.

IX — August

A republic younger than its lanes.

Old Delhi & Agra

Plate 11 · Independence Day Eve The flags go up outside Jama Masjid on the night of the fourteenth. The mosque was finished in 1656. The republic it stands in turns seventy-eight in the morning.

Plate 11½ · The Mughal Inheritance Between 1526 and 1857 the Mughals ruled most of the subcontinent. Six emperors, three centuries, one slow argument between Persian and Indian about what a court could look like. The Taj is the postcard. The deeper inheritance is quieter — Urdu, the four-quartered charbagh, Mughlai food, the formal kitchens of Hindustani music, the idea of the emperor as patron of poets. Empires fall. The grammar they leave behind does not.

X — August

A god born again, on the night of the rains.

Vrindavan, Uttar Pradesh

Plate 12 · Janmashtami In Vrindavan the women dress as gopis and dance the night Krishna was born — a night so often retold it is no longer one night.

XI — October

The goddess goes home by water.

Kolkata, West Bengal

Plate 13 · Visarjan On the tenth day, Durga is carried back to the Hooghly. The river takes the idol; the river takes the year. By morning the ghats are empty and the city has begun to remember her again.

Coda

After the festivals, the small unguarded peace.

Varanasi

Plate 14 · The Sleeping Priest A priest and a stray pup, asleep in the same patch of afternoon. Varanasi has held both — the holy and the homeless, the human and the not-quite-human — for three thousand uninterrupted years. The festivals will come around again. For now: this. A breath. The city's oldest grace. And the end, for this year, of the road.
